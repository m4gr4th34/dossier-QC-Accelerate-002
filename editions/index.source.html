Chapter 2 — What works, what loops, what looms. Robots have worked in labs for forty years; that is not news. The news is the closed loop — AI chooses the experiment, machines run it, results steer the next one, around the clock. This chapter maps where that already works in quantum computing, where it is half-built, what it would take to close the loop on qubits themselves — and the doors no amount of iteration can open. Every label below is exactly as strong as its evidence.

THE CHAPTER · THE ARGUMENT

01WHAT ALREADY WORKS

Start with the floor of documented fact. Google's AlphaQubit decodes the with a neural network — and its successor does it in real time, under a microsecond per round . Learned control pulses design error-robust gates on real superconducting hardware, beating the human-default pulses and staying calibrated for weeks . And reinforcement-learning agents discover error-correcting codes from scratch, scored against the . Reading errors, driving gates, designing codes — AI is in production on three floors of the stack. One discipline before going further: this field is crowded. Automated code search is a populated subfield, and fault tolerance as a phase of matter is decades deep. Any claim of novelty must clear that thicket first; this survey claims none.

02THE THREE RUNGS

Robotics in science is not news — motorized stages, liquid handlers, and fab lines have run for forty years. A robot executing a human's protocol is automation: rung one, prior art by construction. Rung two is the partial loop, and quantum computing already lives there: calibration software closes a loop on live hardware with no arms at all, and cryogenic probers close the measurement half at industrial scale. Rung three is the closed loop — AI chooses the next experiment, machines run it, the result steers the choice after that, and the cycle compounds at machine tempo, around the clock. Rung three is where the timeline-compression bet lives. The honest state of its evidence is the next section. Fig 1 draws the three rungs — watch where the loop waits.

03LOOPS, HALF-CLOSED

The halves exist; the join does not. Intel's cryogenic prober measures hundreds of spin-qubit devices per wafer and feeds the statistics back into fabrication — the measure-half, industrialized . In chemistry the decide-half is proven: a mobile robotic chemist ran a days-long Bayesian-steered search and found a better photocatalyst than its starting point, unattended . And the flagship of autonomous materials discovery is also the cautionary tale: the A-Lab reported 41 novel compounds in 17 unattended days — and after independent re-analysis, Nature published a formal correction: new to the prediction platform, not necessarily new to science . No public system yet closes the full loop on qubit metrics. That gap is exactly where the landscape's boldest card places its bet. Fig 2 draws that assembly — and its one open ring.

04WHERE THE LOOP WAITS

If the closed loop is the bet, honesty owes the reader the bottlenecks — ranked, with tentative horizons. What follows is this program's own reasoning, forecast-grade: the analysis under the landscape's dated cards, not settled fact. Fig 1's dashed arrows mark exactly where each one lives.

One scope line first: this ledger is priced for the solid-state mainline — superconducting and silicon-spin qubits, the platforms where both halves of the loop demonstrably exist today. Shift platforms and the ledger shifts. For trapped ions and neutral atoms the fabrication bottleneck largely vanishes — the qubit is an atom, identical by nature — and the loop becomes a control-optimization problem. For topological qubits every bottleneck below is preceded by a worse one: the learning signal's very meaning is contested (see the doors, below) — and no loop closes on a reward whose interpretation is disputed. And for defect qubits — color centers grown, implanted, annealed — the make-step is materials synthesis, exactly the shape today's autonomous labs already have: if the loop closes first anywhere, that is the plausible venue.

Hardest: fabrication in the loop. Self-driving labs work where the make-step is robot-shaped — liquids, furnaces, powders . A qubit device is a multi-week cleanroom flow across batch tools never designed for unattended iteration, with human judgment at a dozen stations. The near path is constrained loops: fix the process, let the AI iterate only over what a recipe file can change. Tentative horizon: constrained-subspace fabrication loops in roughly three to five years; loops over general device space, sometime in the 2030s. This is the structural ceiling on how much design space any loop can actually reach.

Second: the learning signal. Qubit figures of merit are cruel reward functions — coherence drifts in time on a single device and varies across nominally identical ones, so crediting an improvement to the loop's chosen variable takes statistics over many devices per iteration; that is the wafer-scale prober's real significance . And a loop that characterizes its own outputs is structurally tempted to over-credit itself — the A-Lab's formal correction is the standing lesson . A per-iteration signal trustworthy enough to credit, with independent verification built into the cycle, looks like four to seven years — and it quietly gates the other bottlenecks.

Third: cryogenic tempo. Real coherence lives at millikelvin, and a dilution-refrigerator cycle is days. The working answer — probe warmer, accept proxy metrics, multiplex hundreds of devices per cooldown — is already here and improving: one to three years to routine. The honest cost is that fast loops run on proxies, and the proxy-to-millikelvin correlation must itself be validated — which folds straight back into the signal problem.

Fourth, the unglamorous one: orchestration. Cleanroom tools, probers, refrigerators and analysis stacks were never built to talk to each other, and a loop is only as autonomous as its least scriptable instrument. This is engineering, not physics — the self-driving-lab field solved its version with standard lab-robot interfaces — but someone has to do it for the cryogenic stack: call it two to four years of integration work nobody will write a paper about.

Compound the four and the landscape's card prices itself. A loop credibly credited with an adopted advance by 2031 threads constrained fabrication, statistics-heavy proxies, and independent verification — plausible, not promised. The general dissolving-lab bet sits behind fabrication's long tail, which is why its card carries the later date and the longer odds. The next section follows that tail to its end.

05THE RACE: WHO CLOSES IT FIRST

If the loop is the accelerant, the strategic question is who closes it first — and the honest unit of comparison is not America versus China but the allied bloc versus China. The decide-half's flagship is British, the fridge monopoly was Finnish and British, the export controls are a five-party coalition. What follows is the author's position, labeled as such and carried as a dated forecast in the landscape: conditional on no structural change in how, by whom, and at what scale the United States does research, momentum favors China taking a commanding lead in AI and quantum within a decade.

Score the four bottlenecks first. Fabrication-in-the-loop: China, structurally — the loop's substrate is co-location, and Hefei has already assembled fab, robots, national lab and compute in one city. The learning signal: the allied bloc, clearly — the decoder ecosystem, the statistics engines, and the adversarial-verification culture that makes results credited. Cryogenic tempo: allied today, China's trajectory. Orchestration: China — state-directed integration is precisely the advantage for glue work nobody writes papers about.

Now the rates, because stocks flatter the incumbent and flows tell the truth. Money first. In 2024 China's research spending crossed the United States' on a basis — $1.03 trillion to $1.01 trillion — after two decades growing at over 14% a year, roughly double the US pace; China's government-sector R&D already runs 1.6 times the US level . Adjust for what the money buys and the gap inverts harder: $100,000 of R&D employs 2.3 researchers in China for every one in the United States .

People. China employs more researchers than the United States and the European Union combined and graduates roughly twice the US's STEM doctorates. And the quality flip has already happened at the top: between 2020 and 2024, a forty-thousand-paper bibliometric study counted China's top-cited scientists rising from 18,805 to 32,511 while the American count fell from 36,599 to 31,781 .

Output. China passed the United States in most-cited papers in 2022 and now leads the Nature Index of high-quality journals outright . Quantity arrived a decade ago; these are the quality indicators following the same curve.

And quantum specifically — the rate evidence this chapter cares most about. Zuchongzhi went from 66 qubits in 2021 to a 105-qubit processor whose published sampling task sits six orders of magnitude beyond Google's equivalents ; program claims of below-threshold error correction on that line remain interested-party reports, and are labeled so here. Jiuzhang went from 76 detected photons in 2020 to over three thousand in 2025 — fortyfold in five years. A superconducting machine runs as a commercial cloud with tens of millions of visits; a neutral-atom machine has been exported. And the sharpest rate curve is the one the allied bloc drew itself: fourteen months after five-party controls put dilution refrigerators under presumption-of-denial, China fielded ten-plus domestic makers, factory production near 10 millikelvin, and a published 6.6-millikelvin research system . The loop substrate is the same story: a multi-agent robotic chemist running on an on-board open-weight model, in the same city as the fab and the national lab .

The honest counterweights, weighted honestly. The United States still leads basic research more than twofold as a share of GDP, and federal money buys the world's deepest reservoir of curiosity-driven science . Error correction — the metric that matters most — is where analysts still place China's deficit. Insularity costs ideas: a controlled ecosystem reads fewer preprints than it writes. And the trust discount is real — a loop grading its own homework is the A-Lab lesson , and a Chinese loop's first discoveries will spend years earning reproduction abroad. Nor is the momentum playbook a law of nature: in EUV lithography and jet engines, decades of the same state-directed pursuit have not closed the gap. The prior is a prior, not a destiny.

The verdict splits, and the split is the finding. On raw capability — a 24/7 autonomous loop iterating on qubit-relevant materials and devices — China plausibly first, around 2028 to 2030: its advantages sit exactly on the two most structural bottlenecks. On this chapter's actual signpost — a loop-discovered advance adopted by a leading platform, credited and independently verified — the allied bloc first, around 2029 to 2031: the platforms where adoption cashes out are mostly American, and credited is the allied bloc's home game. China is favored to build the loop; the West is favored to be believed first. The author's larger bet — the decade, the commanding lead, the condition — is carried in the landscape above with its percentage and signpost, where a hostile referee can score it. The condition is not rhetorical: US federal R&D has fallen from 1.86% of GDP in 1964 to roughly 0.6% today, its lowest share since the 1950s . Drastic is the accurate word for what reversing that requires.

PROGNOSISThe United States needs to rethink its R&D — who does it, how it is funded, and at what scale — within this decade, or plan on reading about the closed loop in translation.

Which returns the chapter to its real question — the puck. Everyone is skating to today's puck: superconducting incumbents and their roadmaps. Where the puck is going is the platform where the loop closes first, in the bloc whose results are believed, adopted by the incumbents that matter. The scope note in the bottleneck ledger already named the loop-nearest venue; the race analysis names the blocs. Triangulating those into a platform bet — where to stand before the puck arrives — is the next chapter's opening problem. Fig 3 draws the rates this section argued.

06THE DISSOLVING LAB

If the loop closes, the benefit compounds three ways: cycle time (the loop does not wait for humans), information per cycle ( picks the most informative next experiment, not the next grid point), and unattended hours (the loop does not sleep). Follow that curve and the laboratory changes shape — server farms run the theory programs, robots run the benches, and the scarce human skill shifts from performing the experiment to understanding what the machines report. Posted here as dated bets, not prophecy: the landscape carries both, each with a signpost a hostile referee can score.

07THE DOORS THAT STAY SHUT

None of this touches the conceptual gates. Whether Majorana zero modes exist in InAs-Al devices is a physics question — the claimed readout is published, the critique arguing its diagnostic is unreliable is published, the reply is published, and the question stands open in Nature's own pages . No loop speed settles an existence question; iteration optimizes within a paradigm, it does not supply one. That is the lesson this program sealed in Chapter 1: an AI search handed every freedom recovered the human gate, declined the cheat, and could not move the wall . Faster loops will find better answers inside known physics. The doors that stay shut are where the next chapters knock.
