**THE CHAPTER · THE EXPEDITION**

## 01 THE QUESTION CHAPTER 4 LEFT

Last chapter ended with a promise and a boundary. The boundary: the loop had been turned exactly once — a single propose-refute-repair pass over hand-seeded candidates, hours of compute, a proof of principle. The promise: let an automated proposer run, and see what it finds. This chapter kept the promise, and the honest report is that the interesting thing was not what the proposer found. It was what the grader could not do.

Recall the game. A code, here, is a small grid of 0s and 1s; the machine it implies — qubits, helper qubits, gates, measurements, cycle after cycle — is simulated under noise copied from published cat-qubit hardware [Nature 2025], a decoding algorithm fights every injected error, and each time it loses, that is one logical failure. The number this dossier ranks on is the per-logical, per-round failure rate at a fixed future operating point — the regime the hardware roadmap points at [Nat. Commun. 2025]. One number per code. Lower wins.

The wall is this: for any code good enough to care about, that number is somewhere around one failure in ten million runs. Simulate two hundred thousand runs — a generous budget, minutes to hours per code — and the expected number of failures you observe is zero, one, maybe two. Zero observed failures is not a measurement; it is a floor, a statement that the truth lives somewhere below what you can see. Early in this chapter we watched two strong codes swap places on the leaderboard when the shot budget doubled — not because either code changed, but because two events against one is a coin toss dressed as a ranking. An automated loop steering on numbers like that is steering on noise.

*(figure: The evaluation pipeline from a code grid through the simulated checking machine with injected noise and a decoder, to a single failure-rate number; below it, two budget panels contrasting 200,000 runs (zero to two observed failures, a floor) against 20 million runs (85 to 200 observed failures, a real measurement). — Fig 1 — The game, and the wall. The game: a code (a small grid of 0s and 1s) implies a checking machine; simulate it under chip-calibrated noise with a decoder fighting every fault, and count failures — one number per code, lower wins. The wall: for a strong code the true rate is ~1 failure in 10,000,000 runs, so the field-standard budget of 200,000 runs observes 0-2 events — a floor dressed as a ranking (this chapter watched a leaderboard invert on a re-roll). Seeing the answer took 20,000,000 runs per code: 100x the standard budget. [SCHEMATIC + SCORED numbers as committed])*

So this chapter became, against its own plan, a chapter about measurement. Four campaigns of automated search ran and taught real lessons — that the loop can learn, that one prized find was a hard local optimum, that fitness design is load-bearing — but every campaign ultimately foundered on the same reef: no affordable number honestly ranks strong codes at the operating point. What follows is the story of buying that number: every shortcut killed in the open, two clever instruments built and buried by their own pre-registered kill criteria, and then the unglamorous purchase — sixty-seven million simulated runs in one night — of the answer the chapter was founded to find. Pre-registered priors, committed before each experiment, frame every step; the measurement arc alone registered twenty-five across four documents, eight resolved against us, five died untested when their instruments were killed — and each miss is published at the size of a hit.

## 02 THE WALL, MAPPED

Why can nothing cheap stand in for the honest number? This chapter's first arc tried every candidate and buried each with a cause of death on the certificate.

**Algebra is not the operating point.** A code's vital statistics — block size, encoded qubits, distance — are free to compute and genuinely predictive of *something*, but the something is not the ranking at the operating point. The measured leaderboard at this chapter's end makes the point brutally: a distance-16 code ties a distance-12 code, and a distance-13 code beats them both while a distance-7 code loses to everything. No monotone story in distance survives contact with the table. Rate-per-distance composites, slope fits at elevated noise, threshold crossings — each was gated against measured truths and each died; the elevated-noise fits die of a curvature confound (the ranking at ten times the noise is not the ranking at the operating point, and the extrapolation between them is exactly the quantity in dispute).

**Counting faults is not the axis either.** Here is the mechanism, and it is the chapter's quiet centerpiece. At the operating point, the average simulated run of the error-correction machine suffers about eight point four faults — not one rare fault, eight, every run, because the machine has hundreds of fault locations and each is only mostly reliable. Almost all such runs end fine: the decoder catches everything. Failure is not a run with unusually *many* faults; it is a run whose perfectly typical number of faults land in a conspiring *pattern*. Conditioning on the count buys nothing — we built the exact machinery (this chapter's first instrument) and measured its advantage over brute force at parity: none. The rare event lives in the geometry of which faults, not the arithmetic of how many.

*(figure: A log-scale curve of failure probability versus number of faults per run, rising smoothly from about one in forty-two thousand at six faults to thirty-five percent at thirty-four faults, with a dashed marker at the typical run of eight point four faults where failure chance is about one in seven thousand. — Fig 2 — Why counting faults buys nothing: the measured failure spectrum of one code (rep2 x extended-Hamming) at the operating point — the chance that a run with exactly w faults fails, from 1-in-42,000 at six faults to 35% at thirty-four, on a log axis. The typical run (dashed line) already carries 8.4 faults and fails only ~1 time in 7,000: failures are typical counts in conspiring patterns, not rare pile-ups. [SCORED · numbers as committed])*

That single measured fact — typical counts, conspiring patterns — is the wall in one sentence, and both purpose-built instruments of the next section were attempts to climb it at exactly its two footholds.

## 03 TWO INSTRUMENTS, TWO HONEST DEATHS

**Instrument one: stratify by fault count.** If failures were concentrated at unusual fault counts, sampling each count separately and recombining exactly would beat brute force. The instrument was built to metrological standard — exact stratum weights, exact conditional sampling, enumeration of the cheap strata, every convention imported from the frozen referee rather than re-derived — and gated in three pre-registered steps against directly measured truths before it was allowed to touch an open question. It passed every gate. And then it ran at parity: the same precision as brute force, for the same price, because (section 02) failure does not live on the count axis. The instrument survives as machinery — its exact failure-spectrum measurement is the engine of the next chapter's plan — but as a shortcut it is dead, and the record says so in its own commit messages.

**Instrument two: enumerate the conspiracies.** If failing fault patterns were built from a modest catalogue of minimal "cores" — small conspiring sets that doom any run containing them — then classical rare-event technique (union sampling over the catalogue, with the decoder adjudicating every sample so the estimate stays honest even when extra faults *rescue* a doomed run) would crack the problem. The instrument's estimator was validated unbiased against brute-force exact answers before deployment. Then the catalogue-building loop ran, and delivered a beautiful negative: roughly fifty *never-before-seen* minimal cores per harvest round, three rounds, zero saturation, while the catalogue's coverage of the failure probability sat at 0.14 percent. The arithmetic is short and damning: every failing run provably contains a minimal core, so the complete catalogue's summed firing probabilities must at least equal the failure rate — and at the measured per-core probabilities that demands somewhere between sixteen thousand and thirty million distinct conspiracies. The failure structure is combinatorially diffuse. No enumerable catalogue compresses it. Both registered kill criteria fired inside budget, and the design died of structure, not resources.

The wreckage paid twice. First: the harvest's byproduct was six *exactly known* two-fault patterns — one physical pattern, repeated once per correction cycle — with a property that reframes the whole chapter: the two faults, together, flip **nothing**. The stored information is intact. The syndrome they light up simply fools the decoder into "correcting" its way into a logical error. The failure is the decoder's invention. Readers from classical coding theory will recognize the genus — these are trapping sets [Quantum 2021] — surfacing here at circuit level, under biased cat-qubit noise, with the decoder everyone actually uses. Second: the diffuse-structure result itself, which any future sampling method must now beat on the record, not in principle.

*(figure: Three panels: two harmless idle-window faults that flip nothing; the benign six-detector alarm pattern they produce; and the decoder misreading the alarms and applying a correction that itself flips the logical bit. — Fig 3 — Anatomy of a decoder trap, one of six exactly-known on the gate code: two idle-window faults land (panel 1) that together flip nothing — the stored information is intact; their six-detector alarm pattern (panel 2) is unusual but benign; the decoder reads it, picks the wrong explanation, and applies a correction that flips the logical bit (panel 3). The failure is the decoder's invention. One physical pattern repeats once per correction cycle, giving six traps, each known to the exact fault pair — circuit-level members of the trapping-set genus from classical coding theory. ## 04 BRUTE FORCE, PRICED HONESTLY

With every shortcut dead by measurement, the defensible instrument was the oldest one: simulate at true cost, overnight, with exact Poisson statistics and a rule fixed in advance — no ordering claim unless the 95% intervals do not touch. Five codes. Sixty-seven million runs. Four point seven hours. Every failure counted.

*(figure: A five-row interval chart on a log axis of per-logical failure rate: rep-13 as a leftmost floor arrow at 2.31e-8; the two AI finds tied near 1.3e-7; rep-7 near 6.2e-7; C3 near 2.1e-5; a bracket marks the 4.6x gap between the matched-efficiency pair. — Fig 4 — The leaderboard, measured: five codes at the operating point, 67 million runs, exact 95% intervals, log axis (left is better). Certified orderings only where intervals do not touch: rep-13 (a floor at 2.31e-8, zero failures in 20 million runs) beats everything; the two AI finds are statistically TIED and left tied; rep-7 loses to both. The headline sits in the bracket: the AI find and rep-7 carry exactly the same efficiency (0.0769 logical per physical resource), and at that matched price the find fails 4.6x less often. C3, a weaker find, anchors the right edge. [SCORED · numbers as committed])*

The founding question resolves in one comparison the table makes fair by construction. The automated loop's champion and the human baseline it was always benchmarked against — a seven-qubit repetition code — carry *exactly* the same efficiency: one-thirteenth of a logical qubit per physical resource, 0.0769 to four places, which is precisely why that baseline was chosen chapters ago. At that matched price, the loop's find fails 4.6 times less often: 1.33e-7 against 6.15e-7 per logical per round, intervals cleanly separated. **The loop beats the human pick, at the human pick's own price point, measured.** Three honest qualifications ride with the headline, all on the record. The thirteen-qubit repetition code, at roughly half the efficiency, still holds the outright per-logical crown — its row produced zero failures in twenty million runs, a floor at 2.31e-8 that now stands as the challenge any future find must measure below. The loop's two best codes — distance 12 and distance 16 — are statistically *tied*, and the record leaves them tied. And the old "repetition always dominates" conclusion from earlier campaigns is revealed as a resolution artifact: at the old budgets the seven-qubit code's true failure rate was literally invisible — one expected event — and what cannot be seen cannot lose.

One more number frames everything that follows. The proxy budgets this chapter inherited were two hundred thousand runs per code. The answer cost twenty million per row. The gap between what everyone spends and what the truth costs is two orders of magnitude — and every code-search paper grading itself at the cheap budget is publishing floors, not measurements.

## 05 WHOSE FAULT IS FAILURE?

The trap discovery of section 03 raised a suspicion too important to leave qualitative: if a decoder can invent a logical error out of a harmless pair of faults, how much of every code's failure rate is the decoder's invention? The chapter's last measurement asked exactly that, with a metric chosen for bluntness. Take every failing run and check one thing: did the injected faults, by themselves, actually flip the stored information? If yes, the physics presented a genuine logical event. If *no* — information intact, decoder "corrected" its way into a flip — the failure is decoder-created, full stop. The fraction of decoder-created failures is a hard *lower bound* on the decoder's culpability (the genuine-event majority is left unattributed: some of it is unavoidable ambiguity, some may yet be decoder suboptimality of a subtler kind).

*(figure: A five-row interval chart of decoder-created failure share from zero to one hundred percent with a dashed majority line at fifty percent: four codes sit between eight and thirty-five percent; rep-7 sits at sixty-three percent with an interval spanning the line. — Fig 5 — Whose fault is failure: for each code, the share of operating-point failures where the injected faults flipped nothing and the decoder's own correction did the damage — a hard lower bound on the decoder's culpability, with 95% intervals. A minority on four of five codes (8.5-35%), but spanning sevenfold under the same decoder at the same operating point: the decoder gap is a property of the code-decoder pair, so any decoder repair moves the leaderboard non-uniformly. The indicated exception is rep-7 (point 63%; interval spans the majority line, so indicated, not certified) — the code the headline result beats — which frames the next chapter's registered question: how much of the 4.6x survives fixing the decoder? [SCORED · numbers as committed])*

The answer came back with structure nobody ordered. On four of the five measurable codes the decoder-created share is a **minority** — 8.5 to 35 percent. But it is not one number: it spans those codes by a factor of seven under the *same decoder at the same operating point*, with the extremes certified apart. The decoder gap is not a property of the decoder; it is a property of the code-decoder *pair* — which means any future decoder improvement will move the leaderboard non-uniformly, and a ranking measured under one decoder is, in part, a ranking *of* that decoder. The one indicated exception is the seven-qubit repetition code — the very baseline the headline result beats: its point estimate says sixty-three percent of its failures are decoder-created, though its interval spans the fifty-percent line, so the majority reading is indicated, not certified. Which frames the question the next chapter is built on, registered before any repair is attempted: **how much of the 4.6× survives fixing the decoder?**

## 06 WHERE THIS SITS

Three literatures meet at this chapter, and the intersection was empty when it started. First, AI code search: the field is prolific — reinforcement learning [Quantum 2019] [npj QI 2024], weight-constrained RL [arXiv 2025], LLM-guided evolution and Bayesian optimization in a 2026 flurry [arXiv 2026] [arXiv 2026] — and, checked sentence by sentence, grades itself at code capacity, on budgets where (section 04) strong codes are floors, not measurements. This chapter's campaigns are, as far as we can find, the only published record of a search loop run against the circuit-level operating-point number itself — including the honest reason that is rare: four campaigns' worth of the objective being unmeasurable, ending in the priced answer.

Second, rare-event measurement: three weeks before this chapter's instruments died, the field converged on the same objects. A November 2025 preprint [arXiv 2025] proposes fitting a low-parameter ansatz to the failure spectrum — the exact fraction-of-w-fault-runs-that-fail curve this chapter's first instrument measures with exact stratum weights — plus exact low-weight counting and a splitting method; circuit-level splitting recipes followed [arXiv 2025]. Our diffuse-structure kill and parity result are, read against that program, field notes on where those techniques will and will not save you; our six certified operating-point truths are the calibration set such rulers should be gated against, and the next chapter does exactly that. Third, decoder failure structure: the traps of section 03 are circuit-level members of the trapping-set genus [Quantum 2021], where classical experience says exact knowledge of a decoder's failure sets buys orders of magnitude — under biased cat-qubit noise, with the production decoder, that program has not been run. It is the next chapter's Phase 1. And the leaderboard itself lands in a live debate: the flagship cat-qubit roadmap replaces repetition with high-rate LDPC outer codes [Nat. Commun. 2025]; this chapter's measured table — products beating matched-efficiency repetition, losing to half-efficiency repetition — is a small, certified data point in exactly that trade.

**And here is why any of it matters.** Every quantum-computing roadmap lives or dies on one ratio: physical resources per logical qubit. The cat-qubit lane's flagship claim — a hundred logical qubits on 758 physical cats — is exactly a bet about which outer codes win at which price points, and it is a bet currently made on cheap grading. This chapter built the expensive scale and weighed five contenders honestly; what it is building toward is the thing that would actually move that ratio. Three registered shots, in rising order of impact. First: the six known decoder traps invite a repaired decoder, and the measured code-decoder interaction says repair will re-shuffle the leaderboard — if the repair alone drops the leading codes below today's floor, the ratio moves without touching the hardware. Second: a cheap ruler that survives its gate against this chapter's six certified truths would make honest grading a per-minute cost instead of a per-night one — and that, finally, is a discovery loop that can actually run, steering on the number that matters instead of a proxy that lies. Third, the crown: an AI-discovered code measured below 2.31e-8 at competitive efficiency — beating the best human pick outright, at scale, on the certified instrument. That is the result a stranger would care about. This chapter is not that result. It is the reason the attempt will mean something: the wall is mapped, the instrument is priced, the truths are certified, the question is registered — and when the crown attempt resolves, in either direction, nobody will have to take anyone's word for it.

## 07 THE ERROR LEDGER

Nine catches in this chapter's measurement arc alone, each caught by the discipline built for it, each on the record in the expedition notebook — and four of them are the machinery catching its own author, which is the strongest evidence the machinery works. The author's side: hardcoded "truth" constants that were the strategy room's reconstruction rather than the committed bytes — caught by the landmark-anchor gate on its first firing; a checkpoint count written from a stale mid-run report — caught twice, against the file, by the executing agent; a commit clause asserting records "identical to committed outputs" that had no committed twins — caught by pre-commit re-verification; a "majority" claim whose interval spans the majority line — caught at the diff gate and softened to what the statistics certify. The machinery's side: a registered gate that was arithmetically impossible as written (its budget excluded a cost it mandated) — caught before the run, fixed by dated addendum; a seed-reproducibility claim falsified across machines (the simulator's random streams are per-machine; the numerics library's are not) — corrected append-only; a shell that aborts speculative file checks; a transport channel that corrupts long code (files and hashes ever since); and an approval relayed in the wrong room, twice, caught both times by a one-line guard that now heads every handoff. None of these touched a published number. All of them would have, eventually, somewhere — that is what the ledger is for.

One reproducibility honesty note, learned the hard way and recorded in the notebook: the simulator's seeded random streams are deterministic per machine but not across machines — your leaderboard will agree within the published intervals, not to the byte; the numerics-library paths (all samplers here) reproduce exactly anywhere. The six certified truths are the calibration set for any cheaper ruler you care to propose; the rep-13 floor, 2.31e-8, is the number to beat; and the decoder-gap question is open, registered, and waiting.
