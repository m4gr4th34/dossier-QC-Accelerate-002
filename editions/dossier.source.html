ADVERSARIAL REVIEW

Red-team findings

A hostile pass over the finished survey, published with it. Resolved findings were fixed before launch; open findings are stated as caveats in the avenues themselves.

RESOLVEDEvery number here grades a model, not a chip
The winning code, the one-quarter error ratio, the whole severity map — all are measured inside a circuit-level simulator calibrated to Amazon's Ocelot chip, not on hardware. The label "a model-level result, not a hardware demonstration" rides with the claim on the front door, in the figures, and in the ledger. The evaluator earned that license: it closes on three independently measured Ocelot numbers with no fitting, and matches an independent group's published simulation at twelve of twelve grid points, within about 25% at the operating point. But a validated model is still a model. Stated as the lead finding, not buried at the bottom.
RESOLVEDThe instrument reads 1.2-2x optimistic in absolute terms — disclosed, and cancelled in ratios
Against the same independent published fit, the evaluator sits 1.2-2x low on absolute logical error. Every quantitative conclusion in the chapter is therefore a same-instrument ratio, where the shared optimism cancels; absolute numbers are labeled as such. The tilt is disclosed on the front door and here rather than smoothed away.
RESOLVEDThe loop turned once — a proof of principle, not a search
This chapter ran a single propose-refute-repair pass over a small, hand-seeded candidate bank: the scoring is automated, the proposals were human/LLM-guided. It shows the loop closes and yields a checkable result; it does not exhaust the code space or claim optimality. Framed as a proof of principle in the chapter's first section, with an automated many-generation search named as the next chapter's work — so the reach of the claim matches what was actually run.
RESOLVEDTen of the machinery's own errors — caught, credited, published (E1-E10)
E1 a phantom build environment; E2 reading the published logical error as a sum when the paper defines it as an average; E3 an amplitude-pinned fit biased about 2x low; E4 a corridor observable taken before the n-th root; E5 a fetch-summarizer's wrong arithmetic on a quoted formula; E6 an evaluator that tracked only one of several logical observables; E7 an imprecise pre-run preview sentence; E8 a cross-depth comparison flattered by boundary effects (below); E9 a one-character drift in a handoff, caught by a byte guard; E10 a straight apostrophe that broke a figure caption's JSON on the first bake. Each was caught by the discipline built for exactly that failure, each is credited to its catcher in the expedition notebook. The errors are half the value.
RESOLVEDA depth artifact nearly produced a wrong verdict — now a standing rule
An early cross-code comparison put a sixteen-round candidate against an eight-round comparator. Boundary effects flatter shorter runs (measured: repetition-7 at 16 rounds vs 8 rounds = 1.23), which would have inflated the win. Caught, diagnosed, and closed by re-running depth-matched — and promoted to a standing rule: every cross-code comparison in this chapter runs both codes at matched depth on the same instrument. The verdict that survived is the depth-matched one.
OPENThe GM gate-channel constants are not yet byte-verified against the source PDF
The high-bias gate model uses the CNOT error channel from Guillaud & Mirrahimi (PRX 9, 041053); the numerical constants are carried from the published form but not yet re-derived against the PDF byte-for-byte (OPEN-7). The chapter's comparative conclusions rest on same-instrument ratios and are insensitive to a constant prefactor, but the absolute high-bias numbers would move if a constant is off. Closes on the PDF verification pass.
OPENkappa-2 is not yet pinned from the Ocelot appendices
The high-bias operating point uses the theory loss-to-stabilization ratio kappa-1/kappa-2 = 1e-4; the chip's own two-photon stabilization rate has not yet been extracted from the Ocelot supplementary material (OPEN-1). The operating point is labeled as the search target, not a measured chip value — stated, not hidden.
OPENFive 2026-era code-search papers ride at preprint grade
He & Liu, the IBM LLM-guided search, Liu & Marquardt, the Bayesian-optimization search, and Benito et al. are arXiv preprints, flagged in the citation table. They establish the prior-art landscape — that published AI code search grades at code capacity — rather than carrying any load-bearing number here; each closes as it peer-reviews, or fails to.
OPENOne prior-art noise-model quote is not yet verbatim-confirmed
The Bayesian-optimization paper's noise-model statement is summarized, not yet quoted verbatim from the source; its cite card carries this as open. The seam claim it supports — that AI search and honest circuit-level grading had not previously met — is triangulated across the other four preprints and the one circuit-level counterexample (Benito et al.), not resting on this one source alone.
THE METHOD

The Open Dossier pipeline

The architecture that produced this record — reusable for every subsequent dossier.

  1. Prior-art reconnaissanceExtract candidate novelty claims; search each one, its components, and combinations; produce the established / adjacent / open table.
  2. Claim ledgerEvery claim typed — CITE, NUM, DERIV, NOVEL, EST, FORECAST — routed to its mandated verifier. A FORECAST's verifier is its dated, falsifiable signpost.
  3. DraftingSurvey written against the open ledger. Reproducibility section and AI-assistance disclosure are mandatory.
  4. Mechanical verificationConsistency checks recomputed by script; every citation checked. Fail = fix the paper, never the tolerance.
  5. Adversarial reviewFresh AI sessions briefed as hostile referees. Findings published RESOLVED or OPEN.
  6. Human reviewConsistency and reality checks on end results. Per-claim waiver allowed — at exactly one price: the label tells the truth.
  7. PublicationRepo live → GitHub Pages → OpenTimestamps anchor (auto) on every release → archival milestones additionally Zenodo-DOI'd (this chapter is the dossier's first) → community review via issues.
